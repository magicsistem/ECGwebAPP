Regular Sinus Rhythm

The default heart rhythm. Pacemaking impulses arise from the sino-atrial node and are transmitted to the ventricles via the AV-node and His-Purkinje system. This results in a regular, narrow-complex heart rhythm at 60-100 bpm. Each QRS complex is preceded by a normal P wave. P waves upright in leads I and II, inverted in aVR. The PR interval remains constant.

Treatment

Regular Sinus Rhythm typically requires no specific medical intervention, with recommendations focusing on maintaining overall cardiovascular health through regular check-ups, healthy lifestyle choices, monitoring risk factors such as blood pressure and cholesterol, and proactive preventive care to ensure continued cardiac wellness.

Sinus Bradycardia

Sinus bradycardia is a heart rate of less than 60 beats per minute and originates from the sinus node (as the term "sinus" refers to the sinoatrial node). It has the following characteristics: Rate is less than 60 beats per minute, P Waves precede each QRS complex, PR interval is normal, QRS complex is normal, Conduction is normal, and Rhythm is regular.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Management of Sinus Bradycardia involves comprehensive evaluation of underlying causes, which may include medication review, addressing potential thyroid or metabolic issues, and considering temporary or permanent pacemaker implantation if symptomatic, with ongoing monitoring of heart rate, symptoms like fatigue or dizziness, and potential adjustment of current treatment protocols.

Sinus Tachycardia

Sinus tachycardia is a heart rate greater than 100 beats per minute originating from the sinus node. Characteristics: Rate of 100 to 180 beats per minute, P Waves precede each QRS complex, PR interval is normal, QRS complex is normal, Conduction is normal, and Rhythm is regular. Causes of sinus tachycardia may include exercise, anxiety, fever, drugs, anemia, heart failure, hypovolemia, and shock.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Treatment for Sinus Tachycardia centers on identifying and addressing root causes such as stress, fever, anemia, or hyperthyroidism, potentially utilizing beta-blockers or calcium channel blockers for symptomatic management, implementing lifestyle modifications including stress reduction, hydration, and avoiding stimulants, and conducting regular cardiac evaluations to prevent potential complications.

Sinus Arrhythmia

Sinus rhythm with a beat-to-beat variation in the P-P interval (the time between successive P waves), producing an irregular ventricular rate. Variation in the P-P interval of more than 120 ms (3 small boxes). The P-P interval gradually lengthens and shortens in a cyclical fashion, usually corresponding to the phases of the respiratory cycle. Normal sinus P waves with a constant morphology. Constant P-R interval.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Sinus Arrhythmia generally requires minimal intervention, with clinical approach focusing on comprehensive patient assessment, monitoring for significant symptoms, investigating potential respiratory or cardiac underlying conditions, and conducting regular cardiovascular check-ups to ensure patient health and rule out more serious cardiac complications.

Sinus Exit Block

Sinoatrial exit block is due to failed propagation of pacemaker impulses beyond the SA node. The sinoatrial node continues to depolarise normally. However, some of the sinus impulses are "blocked" before they can leave the SA node, leading to intermittent failure of atrial depolarisation (dropped P waves). The patterns of conduction in the SA exit block are identical to the different types of AV block.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Management of Sinus Exit Block involves detailed cardiac evaluation to identify underlying causes, potential medication adjustments, consideration of pacemaker implantation for persistent cases, comprehensive electrophysiological consultation, and ongoing monitoring to prevent potential hemodynamic complications and ensure appropriate cardiac electrical conduction.

Sinus Arrest

Sudden absence of electrical activity initiated by the SA node. This results in a pause in the electrical activity seen on the tracing. The sinus pause rhythm typically will demonstrate constant R to R intervals prior to and following the pause. Sinus arrest is a pause in the generation of electrical impulses by the SA node. The sinus arrest pause is usually followed by a new sinus node beat or by an AV node escape beat.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Sinus Arrest requires immediate medical intervention, potentially including permanent pacemaker implantation, comprehensive cardiac and systemic condition evaluation, close monitoring of heart function, avoiding high-risk situations that could compromise hemodynamic stability, and regular specialized cardiological follow-up to manage potential recurrence and underlying pathology.

NSR with Premature Atrial Complexes

A premature atrial complex (PAC) is a premature beat arising from ectopic pacemaking tissue within the atria. There is an abnormal P wave, usually followed by a normal QRS complex. AKA: Atrial ectopics, atrial extrasystoles, atrial premature beats, atrial premature depolarizations. Post-extrasystolic pauses may be present, PACs that reach the SA node may depolarise it, causing the SA node to be "reset", with a longer-than-normal interval before the next sinus beat arrives.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Treatment for NSR with Premature Atrial Complexes is typically conservative, focusing on lifestyle modifications such as reducing caffeine and alcohol intake, stress management techniques, potential use of beta-blockers for symptomatic relief, and regular cardiac monitoring to ensure these occasional extra heartbeats do not indicate more significant cardiac issues.

Supraventricular Tachycardia

The term supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) refers to any tachydysrhythmia arising from above the level of the Bundle of His, and encompasses regular atrial, irregular atrial, and regular atrioventricular tachycardias.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Management of Supraventricular Tachycardia involves a multi-faceted approach including acute episode management through vagal maneuvers, potential pharmacological interventions like adenosine, antiarrhythmic medications, consideration of catheter ablation for recurrent cases, patient education on episode recognition and management, and ongoing cardiac monitoring to prevent potential complications.

Atrial Fibrillation

Atrial fibrillation (AF) is the most common sustained arrhythmia. It is characterised by disorganised atrial electrical activity and contraction. The incidence and prevalence of AF is increasing. Lifetime risk over the age of 40 years is ~25%. Complications of AF include haemodynamic instability, cardiomyopathy, cardiac failure, and embolic events such as stroke. It is characterized by: Irregularly irregular rhythm, no P waves, absence of an isoelectric baseline, variable ventricular rate, QRS complexes usually < 120ms, unless pre-existing bundle branch block, accessory pathway, or rate-related aberrant conduction.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Comprehensive Atrial Fibrillation treatment requires a strategic approach involving rate and rhythm control medications, anticoagulation therapy to prevent stroke, potential cardioversion, catheter ablation for refractory cases, lifestyle modifications, management of underlying conditions, regular anticoagulation monitoring, and comprehensive stroke risk assessment.

Atrial Flutter

Atrial flutter is a type of abnormal heart rhythm, or arrhythmia. It occurs when a short circuit in the heart causes the upper chambers (atria) to pump very rapidly. Atrial flutter is important not only because of its symptoms but because it can cause a stroke that may result in permanent disability or death.

Audio Example:

Treatment

When presenting atrial flutter medications like beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, or antiarrhythmic drugs are recommended to control heart rate and rhythm. Anticoagulants are highly used to prevent stroke. Another treatment is cardioversion or electric shock therapy, used to restore normal rhythm. Suggested recommendations include maintaining a healthy lifestyle, avoid excessive alcohol, and monitoring for symptoms like palpitations or dizziness.

Paced Atrial Rhythm

Atrially paced patients often have evidence of 1st degree AV block or Wenckebach conduction on their paced ECG that is not apparent on their baseline tracing. This is because the sort of patients that require atrial pacing (e.g. post-op cardiac surgery) commonly have some degree of AV node dysfunction (e.g. due to age-related AV-nodal degeneration / their underlying cardiac condition / post-operative ischaemia / AV-nodal blocking drugs).

Audio Example:

Treatment

Paced atrial rhythm typically requires no treatment if the pacing is effective and the patient is asymptomatic. Regular pacemaker checks should be performed to ensure proper functioning. In addition, the pacemaker programming should be assessed to optimize performance. Monitoring for any underlying rhythm disturbances that could affect the patient's condition is also recommended.

NSR with First Degree AV Block

First-degree atrioventricular (AV) block is a condition of abnormally slow conduction through the AV node. It is defined by electrocardiogram (ECG) changes that include a PR interval of greater than 0.20 without disruption of atrial to ventricular conduction. This condition is generally asymptomatic and discovered only on routine ECG.

Audio Example:

Treatment

First-degree AV block usually does not require treatment if the patient is asymptomatic. Reversible causes, such as medication effects or electrolyte imbalances, should be addressed. Routine monitoring is advised to detect any progression to more advanced forms of AV block. Regular follow-ups can help manage potential complications effectively.

Second Degree AV Block Type II

In Mobitz type II there is a constant PR interval across the rhythm strip both before and after the non-conducted atrial beat. Each P wave is associated with a QRS complex until there is one atrial conduction or P wave that is not followed by a QRS. Mobitz type II is often a problem in the infra-nodal conduction system, and therefore, is associated with a widened QRS complex, bundle-branch block, or fascicular block.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Second-degree AV block Type II typically requires the implantation of a permanent pacemaker due to its potential to progress to complete heart block. Any underlying conditions, such as ischemia or electrolyte imbalances, should be treated promptly. Immediate intervention is critical in these cases, and AV node-blocking drugs should be avoided.

Second Degree AV Block with 2:1 Conduction

2:1 atrioventricular block is a form of second-degree AV nodal block and occurs when every other P wave is not conducted through the AV node to get to the ventricles, and thus every other P wave is not followed by a QRS complex.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Second-degree AV block with 2:1 conduction often necessitates the placement of a permanent pacemaker, particularly if the patient is symptomatic or the QRS complex is wide. It is important to evaluate and treat reversible causes, as well as to determine whether the block is Type I or Type II. Close monitoring is advised to prevent progression to a more severe block.

Third Degree AV Block

Third-degree atrioventricular (AV) block, also referred to as third-degree heart block or complete heart block (CHB), is an abnormal heart rhythm resulting from a defect in the cardiac conduction system in which there is no conduction through the atrioventricular node (AVN), leading to complete dissociation of the atria and ventricles.

Premature Junctional Complex (PJC)

A premature beat arising from an ectopic focus within the Atrioventricular (AV) junction. Groups of pacemaker cells throughout the conducting system are capable of spontaneous depolarisation. The rate of depolarisation decreases from top to bottom: fastest at the sinoatrial node; slowest within the ventricles. Ectopic impulses from subsidiary pacemakers are normally suppressed by more rapid impulses from above.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Premature junctional complexes are often benign and do not require treatment if infrequent and asymptomatic. Lifestyle modifications, such as reducing caffeine intake and managing stress, can help minimize triggers. For frequent or symptomatic PJCs, Holter monitoring may be used to assess the frequency and impact of the arrhythmia. Reassurance is typically sufficient for benign cases.

Junctional Rhythm

A junctional rhythm is a type of arrhythmia (irregular heartbeat). If you have a junctional rhythm, your heart's natural pacemaker, known as your sinoatrial (SA) node, isn't working as it should. The SA node sends electrical signals that control your heartbeat. When you have a junctional rhythm, your SA node stops working or sends signals that are too slow or weak.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Junctional rhythm requires no treatment if asymptomatic. If symptoms like bradycardia occur, addressing the underlying cause, such as myocardial ischemia or digitalis toxicity, is important. In symptomatic cases, pacing may be considered. It is also advisable to avoid medications that suppress the AV node in patients experiencing symptoms. Regular monitoring for underlying structural heart disease is recommended to ensure proper management.

Accelerated Junctional

Accelerated junctional rhythm (AJR) occurs when the rate of an AV junctional pacemaker exceeds that of the sinus node. This situation arises when there is increased automaticity in the AV node coupled with decreased automaticity in the sinus node.

Audio Example:

Treatment

AJR In most cases, it does not require specific treatment and is usually resolved by correcting triggering factors such as digoxin toxicity, electrolyte imbalances (hyperkalemia, hypomagnesemia) or myocardial infarction. If the patient is symptomatic, intravenous fluids can be administered to maintain blood pressure and, in some cases, beta-blockers or calcium channel blockers to reduce sympathetic activity. The use of atropine is not recommended, as it may increase AV node automaticity and worsen the arrhythmia.

Junctional Tachycardia

Junctional tachycardia is a cardiac arrhythmia originating in the atrioventricular (AV) node, which acts as a secondary natural pacemaker when the sinoatrial (SA) node is damaged and cannot initiate the heartbeat. Under normal conditions, the SA node initiates the electrical impulse that regulates normal sinus rhythm. However, if this node is damaged, the AV node can take over control of the heart rhythm.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Recovery after catheter ablation is usually rapid. It is recommended to avoid strenuous physical exertion for several days. The physician will provide specific instructions according to each patient's particular case. To reduce the risk of developing junctional tachycardia, certain medications may be administered before or during surgery. These include magnesium, propranolol (Inderal¬Æ XL or InnoPran¬Æ XL), and dexmedetomidine, the latter used in some children around cardiac surgical procedures.

Wandering Pacemaker

Wandering atrial pacing is an arrhythmia in which multiple ectopic foci within the atrium generate consecutive electrical impulses that are conducted to the ventricles. The heart rate in this condition remains within a normal range, i.e., below 100 beats per minute. On the electrocardiogram, it is observed that each QRS complex is preceded by a P wave, but with different morphologies, since the impulses come from different atrial regions.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Wandering atrial pacemaker is generally a benign and asymptomatic condition that does not require specific treatment. However, if symptoms persist or affect the patient's quality of life, beta-blockers may be considered to control the heart rate and alleviate discomfort. It is essential that any intervention be supervised by a healthcare professional, who will determine the most appropriate approach. In symptomatic cases, drugs such as beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers and antiarrhythmics may be used.

Sinus Rhythm with Premature Ventricular Complex

Premature ventricular contractions (PVCs) are extra heartbeats that originate in the ventricles, the lower chambers of the heart, and occur before the normal expected heartbeat. These contractions disrupt the regular heart rhythm and may cause you to feel as if your heart has skipped a beat or is beating more forcefully. Although PVCs are common and generally harmless, they can cause symptoms such as palpitations or a feeling that the heart is beating irregularly.

Audio Example:

Treatment

In this case, treatment is recommended to improve the quality of life. However, if the PVCs are very frequent, treatment is offered not only to improve quality of life but to prevent heart failure and, in some cases, to reverse heart failure. If the PVCs are of a more sustained nature (ventricular tachycardia), therapy is directed not only to reduce the PVC burden but also to manage a possible underlying abnormal cardiac substrate.

Idioventricular Rhythm (IVR)

Idioventricular rhythm is a regular, slow ventricular rhythm, with a rate of less than 50 beats per minute, absence of P waves and a prolonged QRS interval. When the sinoatrial node is blocked or depressed, the latent pacemakers are activated to generate a rhythm secondary to the increased activity and produce escape beats that may be atrial, junctional or ventricular.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Rarely, a patient may present with symptoms and not tolerate idioventricular rhythm due to atrioventricular dyssynchrony, a rapid ventricular rate, or degenerated ventricular fibrillation of idioventricular rhythm. Medical therapy may be useful in patients with biventricular failure to restore the atrial impulse by increasing the sinus rate and atrioventricular (AV) conduction. In these cases, atropine may be tried. Antiarrhythmic agents, such as amiodarone and lidocaine, may also be useful along with drugs such as verapamil or isoproterenol. In very rare cases, the use of an atrial pacemaker may be considered.

Accelerated Idioventricular Rhythm (AIVR)

Accelerated idioventricular rhythm is an arrhythmia in which an ectopic pacemaker in the ventricles generates impulses at a rate higher than that of the sinus node, usually between 50 and 120 beats per minute. This rhythm usually presents a regular pattern with wide QRS complexes (greater than 120 ms) and may include fusion and capture beats.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Although it is usually a benign, self-limited arrhythmia that does not require specific treatment, it is important to address the underlying causes, such as correcting electrolyte imbalances or restoring myocardial perfusion. Treatment is generally not necessary. However, in cases where it causes hemodynamic instability, atropine may be considered to increase the sinus rate and suppress the ventricular rhythm. It is also recommended to correct possible triggering factors, such as electrolyte imbalances (hypokalemia or hypomagnesemia) and myocardial ischemia.

Ventricular Tachycardia

Ventricular tachycardia (VT) is an arrhythmia characterized by rapid heartbeats originating in the ventricles of the heart, which prevents efficient pumping of oxygenated blood to the body. It can manifest with symptoms such as palpitations, dizziness, fainting, chest pain and shortness of breath. Causes include structural heart disease, such as previous heart attacks, cardiomyopathies and valvular heart disease, as well as genetic conditions such as catecholaminergic polymorphic ventricular tachycardia.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Treatment options include radiofrequency ablation, a minimally invasive procedure that destroys the cells responsible for the arrhythmia; implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD) implantation, which detects and corrects dangerous heart rhythms; and antiarrhythmic drugs such as sotalol, flecainide, propafenone and amiodarone to prevent recurrences.

Ventricular Fibrillation

Ventricular fibrillation is a dangerous arrhythmia in which disorganized electrical signals cause the ventricles of the heart to contract chaotically, preventing blood from being pumped effectively to the rest of the body. This condition can cause loss of consciousness and, if not treated immediately, lead to sudden cardiac arrest. The most common causes are recent or ongoing heart attacks, cardiomyopathies, heart valve disease, and electrolyte imbalances.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Immediate treatment of ventricular fibrillation is crucial and begins with cardiopulmonary resuscitation to maintain blood circulation, followed by electrical defibrillation to restore normal heart rhythm. After stabilization, antiarrhythmic drugs can be administered to prevent recurrences and the implantation of an implantable cardioverter defibrillator that monitors and corrects abnormal heart rhythms can be considered.

Paced Ventricular Rhythm

A ventricular-paced rhythm refers to the electrical activity of the heart controlled by an artificial pacemaker that sends electrical impulses to the ventricle to regulate its contraction.

Audio Example:

Treatment

Management of a paced ventricular rhythm involves regular monitoring to ensure proper pacemaker function and evaluation of possible symptoms such as fatigue, dizziness, or dyspnea, which may indicate the need to adjust the device's programming. It is essential that patients with pacemakers maintain regular medical follow-up to monitor device function, make adjustments as needed, and address any symptoms or complications that may arise.
